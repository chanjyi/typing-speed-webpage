Keyboard

A computer keyboard is an input device used to enter characters and functions into the computer system by pressing buttons, or keys. It is the primary device used to enter text. A keyboard typically contains keys for individual letters, numbers and special characters, as well as keys for specific functions.

Introduction of Keyboards

The QWERTY keyboard layout gets its name from the first six letters in the top row of keys. It was designed by Christopher Sholes and first introduced in 1873 with the Remington No. 1 typewriter. The layout was created to address the mechanical limitations of early typewriters, which were prone to jamming if certain letter combinations were typed too quickly. Sholes rearrange the keys to slow down typists and prevent adjacent keys from striking the paper at the same time, which would cause the typewriter arms to jam.

The QWERTY keyboard layout was created in the 1860s by Christopher Sholes, a newspaper editor and printer, along with his colleagues Samuel Soule and Carlos Glidden. They developed the layout for the Sholes and Glidden typewriter, which is often considered the first commercially successful typewriter. The design of the QWERTY layout was driven by a specific set of challenges and goals:

  • Jamming Prevention: One of the primary reasons for creating the QWERTY layout was to address the mechanical limitations of early typewriters. Mechanical typewriters had typebars (metal arms with characters on the ends) that swung up to strike the paper and leave an imprint. If adjacent typebars were pressed in quick succession, they could jam the machine. The QWERTY layout was designed to reduce the likelihood of jamming by placing frequently used letter combinations apart from each other.

  • Typist Speed: The QWERTY layout was intended to slow down typists who were too fast, rather than to speed them up. Sholes believed that if common letter pairs were spaced farther apart, it would prevent typists from typing too quickly and jamming the machine.

  • Patent Considerations: The creators of the QWERTY layout also had to consider patents and intellectual property rights. They needed to differentiate their keyboard layout from existing designs to secure a patent for their typewriter. The layout of the keys was part of their patent application.

  • Ease of Learning: While not the primary goal, the creators also aimed to make the layout reasonably easy to learn. They wanted to make sure typists could become proficient with the new technology.

The Dvorak Simplified Keyboard, commonly known as the Dvorak keyboard or Dvorak layout, was created in the 1930s by Dr. August Dvorak, an American psychologist, and his brother-in-law Dr. William Dealey. The Dvorak layout was designed as an alternative to the QWERTY keyboard layout with the primary goal of improving typing efficiency, speed, and ergonomics. The creators believed that the QWERTY layout, which had been designed for mechanical typewriters, was not optimized for modern typists using electric and later electronic keyboards. The reasons behind the creation of the Dvorak keyboard include:

  • Efficiency and Speed: Dr. Dvorak and Dr. Dealey aimed to create a keyboard layout that would increase typing speed and reduce finger movement. They conducted extensive research into letter frequency, finger movement, and typing patterns to design a layout that placed the most common letters on the home row and minimized finger travel.
  • Ergonomics: The Dvorak layout was designed with ergonomics in mind. It aimed to reduce strain on the fingers and hands by placing frequently used keys on the stronger and more dexterous fingers. This design philosophy was based on the idea that a layout optimized for human anatomy would lead to less fatigue and better overall comfort during typing.
  • Reduced Errors: The layout was designed to minimize typing errors by placing commonly used letter combinations in easily accessible positions. This was intended to improve accuracy and reduce the need for corrections.
  • Learning Curve: While the transition from QWERTY to the Dvorak layout requires learning a new keyboard layout, the creators believed that the Dvorak layout's logical arrangement and ergonomic design would make the learning process relatively straightforward and worthwhile for typists in the long run.
  • Technological Advancements: By the time the Dvorak layout was developed, mechanical typewriters had given way to electric and electronic keyboards. The creators believed that these technological advancements allowed for more efficient layouts to be designed without the constraints of mechanical jamming.

Despite its innovative design and the potential benefits it offered, the Dvorak layout did not gain the same level of widespread adoption as the QWERTY layout. This was due to various factors, including the established dominance of QWERTY, resistance to change, and the costs associated with transitioning to a new layout. However, the Dvorak layout continues to have a following among typists who appreciate its ergonomic design and potential for increased typing efficiency.
